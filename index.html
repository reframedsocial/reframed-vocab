Pronoun Cases
  • Subject: I, you, he, she, it, we, they — used as the subject of a verb
  • Object: me, you, him, her, it, us, them — used as object of a verb or preposition
  • Possessive: my/mine, your/yours, his, her/hers, its, our/ours, their/theirs — no apostrophe
To choose between "I" and "me": remove the other person — "She gave it to John and I" → remove "John and" → "She gave it to I" (wrong) → use "me."
✍️ Punctuation
▼
MarkUse
Period (.)Ends a declarative or imperative sentence
Comma (,)Separates list items; joins independent clauses with a conjunction; follows introductory elements; sets off non-essential information
Semicolon (;)Connects two related independent clauses; used in complex lists where items contain commas

Oxford Comma & Key Comma Rules
  • Oxford comma: use a comma before the final item in a list — "red, white, and blue"
  • Introductory element: comma follows — "Although it rained, we went outside."
  • Non-essential clause: surrounded by commas — "My brother, who lives in Paris, called."
  • FANBOYS: comma before for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so when joining independent clauses
American English: periods and commas go inside quotation marks. British English: only if they are part of the quoted material.
🏗️ Sentence Structure
▼
TypeStructureExample
SimpleOne independent clause"The cat sat."
CompoundTwo independent clauses joined by a conjunction or semicolon"The cat sat, and the dog stood."
ComplexOne independent clause + one or more dependent clauses"Although it rained, we went outside."
Compound-complexTwo+ independent clauses + at least one dependent clause"Although it rained, we went outside, and we had a great time."
Common Structural Errors
  • Fragment: missing a subject or verb — "Running through the park." Fix: add what's missing.
  • Run-on: two independent clauses with nothing between them — fix with a period, semicolon, or conjunction.
  • Comma splice: two independent clauses joined by only a comma — add a conjunction or use a semicolon.
  • Dangling modifier: a phrase that doesn't attach to the right noun — "Having finished the report, the meeting began." Who finished it?
  • Broken parallel structure: items in a series must match grammatically — "hiking, swimming, and to run" → "hiking, swimming, and running."
⚠️ Commonly Misused Words
▼
WordsRule
affect / effectAffect is usually the verb (to influence); effect is usually the noun (the result). "The rain affected our plans; the effect was a delay."
their / there / they'reTheir = possession; there = place or existence; they're = they are
its / it'sIts = possessive (no apostrophe); it's = it is
your / you'reYour = possessive; you're = you are
fewer / lessFewer for countable nouns (fewer words, fewer people); less for uncountable quantities (less water, less time)
who / whomWho = subject; whom = object. Test: replace with he/him — if "him" fits, use "whom."
lay / lieLay = to place something (needs an object: "Lay the book down"); lie = to recline ("I need to lie down"). Past tense: laid / lay
that / whichThat introduces essential clauses (no comma); which introduces non-essential clauses (preceded by a comma)
imply / inferThe speaker implies; the listener infers
should ofAlways wrong — it's a mishearing of "should've" (should have)
comprise / composeThe whole comprises the parts; the parts compose the whole. Avoid "comprised of."
🔠 Capitalization Rules
▼
  • First word of a sentence — always capitalize
  • Proper nouns — specific people, places, organizations, brands: London, Nike, Shakespeare
  • Titles before names — "President Biden" — but lowercase after: "Joe Biden, president"
  • Days and months — Monday, January — but not seasons: spring, autumn
  • The pronoun "I" — always capitalized
  • Titles of works — capitalize major words; lowercase articles, short prepositions, and conjunctions unless first word
  • Direct quotations — capitalize the first word of a fully quoted sentence
  • Acronyms — typically all caps: NASA, CEO, FAQ
Don't capitalize for emphasis — use italics or bold instead. Overcapitalization is a common business writing mistake: "Our Company values Innovation and Excellence."
🔢 Numbers in Writing
▼
  • Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above (AP/journalistic style)
  • Never start a sentence with a numeral — spell it out or rewrite the sentence
  • Always use numerals for: percentages (8%), ages (she is 4), measurements, times with a.m./p.m., statistics
  • Be consistent when comparing: "the team had 3 wins and 14 losses" — not "three wins and 14 losses"
  • Large round numbers: "about fifty people attended" vs. "exactly 47 people attended"
  • Decades: the 1990s (no apostrophe) or the '90s (apostrophe replaces "19")
  • Currency: $5, $5.50, $5 million — don't write "$5 dollars" (redundant)
  • Fractions: spell out simple fractions in text — "two-thirds of respondents" — use numerals in technical contexts

🎯 Tone & Style
▼
Active vs. Passive Voice
  • Active: subject acts — "She wrote the report." Clearer and more direct.
  • Passive: subject receives action — "The report was written by her." Use when the actor is unknown or unimportant.

Formality Spectrum
  • Casual: contractions, colloquialisms, first person — social media, friendly emails
  • Conversational: approachable but polished — blogs, newsletters, brand voice
  • Professional: clear, precise, no slang — reports, business writing
  • Formal/Academic: no contractions, third person — research papers, legal documents
Avoid over-hedging in confident writing. "It seems that perhaps this might possibly be worth considering" — cut to "This is worth considering."
